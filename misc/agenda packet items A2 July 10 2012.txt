I. Introduction and Summary

Purpose of report and study objective

The purpose of this document is to provide a traffic analysis study consistent with the
Virginia Department of Transportation (VDOT) Chapter 527 requirements. The flow of
the report is modeled after the "Organization of a Traffic Impact Analysis Report" as
summarized in the Chapter 527 Administrative Guidelines for the Required elements of
a Traffic Impact Analysis (24 VAC 30-155-60. C).

The objective of the study is to report the existing traffic volumes in the study area,
calculate new trips associated with the proposed development, and then identify related
traffic impacts, recommendations to mitigate the impacts, and appropriate strategies for
site access.

This report includes analysis of existing conditions, future no-build for years 2021 and
horizon year 2027, and future build for year 2021 and horizon year 2027.

Executive Summary

Site location and study area. The proposed site is located in both Albemarle County and
the City of Charlottesville, at the end of the Pen Park Lane. For this study, we examined
traffic impacts at the following intersections:

x   E. Rio Road at Pen Park Lane
x   Pen Park Lane at Woodmont

Description of the proposed development.
The proposed development includes 33.5 acres, is by-right at R-4 (site plan submittal)
in the County, and currently R-2 zoning and rezoning to PUD (rezoning submittal) in
the City. Development will include approximately 200 new dwelling units - 130 single
family detached and 70 towns/cottages. Access is all from Pen Park Lane. The project
will be built in 5 distinct phases through year 2021.

       PrincipalFindings. 
       The analysis leads to the following principal findings and recommendations:

       Under the no build condition Pen Park Lane egress left turns are projected to be
       nearly 100 seconds of delay in year 2021. Under the build condition 2021, the
       delays exceed 300 seconds of delay for the westbound approach. Lefts from
       Rio are LOS B or better. Right turn movements generally do not experience
       significant delay, except for the westbound approach where they are delayed by
       the left turning vehicles.

    A traffic signal would relieve the delay on the sidestreet. However, per
    inspection of the MUTCD signal warrants, it appears that only the peak hour
    warrant, and perhaps two hours of the four hour warrant, will be satisfied. A full
    eight hour warrant will not likely be satisfied by the residential traffic patterns
    and volumes. If a lesser density is achieved as compared to what was analyzed
    in this document, then the chances of warranting a signal based on the volume
    warrants decreases further.

    If a traffic signal were to be installed, the intersection the level of service would
    be B, though there could potentially be long queues on Rio Road in the peak
    hours of the day, similar to what has typically be experienced at Pen Park Road
    or Melbourne intersection. Although, through coordination of the traffic
    signals this "middle" location in the system could process the platoons of traffic
    in such as a way that we would expect less queuing at this location than either
    Pen Park Road or Melbourne.

    It appears that the Woodmont/ Pen Park Lane intersection will function
    satisfactorily as a STOP control intersection as it currently exists. In the AM
    peak hour the queues along Pen Park Lane might block the Woodmont
    approach.

    Conclusions

    As noted in the findings section, the intersection will experience high levels of
    delay for the sidestreet under both the build and no-build conditions, though
    under the build condition the queuing will be much more substantial. Similar
    to today, the mainline volumes are projected to be at levels that will make it
    difficult for vehicles entering from the sidestreet to find a gap in the traffic.

    It does not appear that signalization will be fully warranted based on the future
    sidestreet volumes, although the intersection and corridor should be monitored
    in the future and re-examined once the site begins to be occupied.

    Recommendations

    Monitor future volume growth and delays for potential future conditions that
    could warrant traffic signalization.

    Construct a westbound right turn lane from Pen Park Lane should the
    opportunity arise to secure the required right-of-way.

    It might be necessary, and worth considering, a left turn prohibition out of Pen
    Park Lane during peak hours of the day, at some point in the future.

III. Analysis of Existing Conditions
A. Collected daily and peak hour of the generator traffic volumes, tabulated and
presented on diagrams with counts provided in an appendix
Currently, Rio Road and Pen Park Lane are the only existing roads within the study area.
Most of traffic volumes on Rio Road are through traffic to the north and south, but there
is small volume of traffic turning to/from Pen Park Lane. Also, there is a new
connection to Rio Road across from Pen Park Lane that serves a new multifamily
residential development ­ Treesdale and Stonewater. At present there are a small
number of construction trucks to the construction sites of Stonewater and Treesdale
Apartments at the west.

Per the preceding description, the Meadow Creek Parkway, which will affect the traffic
conditions on Rio Road, is now partly open (county portion) and partly under
construction (city portion). The project consists of three elements: a county portion,
which was completed and cleared to open on October 13, 2011; the city's portion,
McIntire Road Extended, which is under construction; and the Meadow Creek Parkway
Interchange, which is also in the city to connect the city's portion of the road at its
southern terminus, but will not proceed until a lawsuit filed by the Coalition to Preserve
McIntire Park against the Federal Highway Administration is resolved.

There are also two residential developments in the study areas that are under
construction: Stonewater Plan, with 40 Single Family Dwellings and 15 Townhouses,

VIII.  RecommendedImprovements

A. Description and diagram of the location, nature, and extent of the proposed
   improvements, with preliminary cost estimates as available from VDOT

Based on the projected peak hour traffic volumes, it appears that the overall average
daily traffic volumes may increase to over 20,000 vehicles per day within the study area
by year 2021 and remain relatively steady since this is pretty much the maximum
capacity of a two lane roadway.

In the model we see that movements to and from the sidestreet (Pen Park Lane) will be
very difficult without signalization of the intersection. With a signal, we see that the
Rio Road movements will function at LOS B or better and the sidestreets will function
at LOS E or better.

Based on the queuing projections, the existing turn lane lengths are satisfactory.

   Additionalfindings:

If possible in the future, the Pen Park Lane approach to Rio Road should be widened to
two egress lanes. At present this is not possible due to adjacent residences, but if the
opportunity arises in the future to acquire these properties it would be advisable to build
the 2nd egress lane.

The Pen Park intersection with Woodmont remains a relatively low volume intersection
and will function at an acceptable LOS in the future. However, the queuing along the
Pen Park Lane approach to Rio Road, in the peak hours of the day, could back up to
Woodmont such that it will be difficult to move from Woodmont onto Pen Park Lane.

Given the full build density, the peak hour warrant and two hours of the 4-hour warrant
may be satisfied, though it is doubtful that the full 4-hour or 8-hour warrants would be
satisfied. If a lesser density is achieved, then it is likely that none of the volume based
signal warrants would be satisfied.
