INTRODUCTION
The purpose of this study is to evaluate the traffic impact that will result from the Mixed-Use
Development proposed along SR 9 (Roswell Road) to the northwest of the intersection of SR 9
(Roswell Road) / Hammond Drive in Sandy Springs, Georgia. The development is proposed to
consist of 6,409 s.f. of retail space and a 273 unit mid-rise apartment. The location of the
proposed development and the surrounding roadway network is shown in Figure 1.

The development is proposed to have three full access driveways (two residential driveways and
one retail driveway) along Sandy Spring Place and one right-in / right-out driveway along
Hammond Drive. In this study, the AM and PM peak hours have been analyzed. This study
includes the evaluation of the existing and future traffic operations at the intersections of SR 9
(Roswell Road) / Hammond Drive, SR 9 (Roswell Road) / Sandy Springs Place, Sandy Springs
Place / Kroger Backside Driveway. In addition, the proposed full access site driveway
intersections along Sandy Springs Place have been evaluated for the future condition.
Recommendations to improve traffic operations have been identified where appropriate.

EXISTING FACILITIES
An inventory was performed of the major roadways in the area surrounding the site. The
following is a brief description of each of these facilities.

SR 9 (Roswell Road)

SR 9 is a north-south five-lane roadway which includes a two-way turn lane with a posted speed
limit of 35 mph in the vicinity of the site.

Hammond Drive

Hammond Drive is an east-west four lane undivided roadway with a posted speed limit of 35
mph. It runs between Ashford Dunwoody Road in the east and Mt. Vernon Highway in the west.

Sandy Springs Place

Sandy Springs Place is a two-lane undivided roadway with a posted speed limit of 25 mph. It
runs between SR 9 (Roswell Road) in the east and Sandy Springs Circle in the west.

STUDY METHODOLOGY
In this study, the methodology used for evaluating traffic operations at each of the subject
intersections is based on the criteria set forth in the Transportation Research Board's Highway
Capacity Manual, 2000 edition (HCM 2000). Synchro software, which utilizes the HCM 2000
methodology, was used for the analysis. The following is a description of the methodology
employed for the analysis of unsignalized and signalized intersections.

Unsignalized Intersections

For unsignalized intersections at which the side street or minor street is controlled by a stop sign,
the criteria for evaluating traffic operations are the level of service (LOS) for the turning
movements at the intersection and the level of service for the overall intersection. Level of
service is based on the average controlled delay incurred at the intersection. Controlled delay for
unsignalized intersections includes initial deceleration delay, queue move-up time, stopped
delay, and final acceleration delay. Several factors affect the controlled delay for unsignalized
intersections, such as the availability and distribution of gaps in the conflicting traffic stream,
critical gaps, and follow-up time for a vehicle in the queue.

Level of service is assigned a letter designation from A through F. Level of service A indicates
excellent operations with little delay to motorists, while level of service F exists when there are
insufficient gaps of acceptable size to allow vehicles on the side street to cross safely, resulting
in extremely long total delays and long queues. The level of service criteria for two-way stop-
controlled and all-way stop-controlled (unsignalized) intersections is given in Table 1.

Signalized Intersections

For signalized intersections, it is necessary to evaluate both capacity and level of service in order
to evaluate the overall operation of the intersection. The capacity analysis of an intersection is
performed by comparing the volume of traffic using the various lane groups at the intersection to
the capacity of those lane groups. This results in a volume/capacity (v/c) ratio for each lane
group. A v/c ratio greater than 1.0 indicates that the volume of traffic has exceeded the capacity
available, resulting in a temporary excess of demand. Although the capacity of the entire
intersection is not defined, a composite v/c ratio for the sum of the critical lane groups within the
intersection is computed. This composite v/c ratio is an indication of the overall intersection
sufficiency.

Level of service for a signalized intersection is defined in terms of average controlled delay per
vehicle, which is composed of initial deceleration delay, queue move-up time, stopped delay, and
final acceleration delay. The levels of service criteria for signalized intersections, based on
average controlled delay, are shown in Table 2. Level of service A indicates operations with very
low controlled delay, while level of service F describes operations with extremely high average
controlled delay. Level of service E is typically considered to be the limit of acceptable delay,
and level of service F is considered unacceptable by most drivers.



                                         Table 2
                  Level of Service Criteria for Signalized Intersections
                          Level of Service           Controlled Delay Per Vehicle (sec)

                                 A                                 10.0
                                 B                           > 10.0 and 20.0
                                 C                           > 20.0 and 35.0
                                 D                           > 35.0 and 55.0
                                 E                           > 55.0 and 80.0
                                 F                                > 80.0



                             Source: 2000 Highway Capacity Manual

EXISTING TRAFFIC OPERATIONS
Existing peak hour traffic counts and intersection geometric data were collected at the
intersections of SR 9 (Roswell Road) / Hammond Drive, SR 9 (Roswell Road) / Sandy Springs
Place, Sandy Springs Place / Kroger Backside Driveway during the AM and PM peak hours.
Turning movement counts were collected on Wednesday, September 21, 2005. All turning
movement counts were recorded during the AM and PM peak hours between 7:00 a.m. and 9:00
a.m. and between 4:00 p.m. and 6:00 p.m., respectively. The four consecutive 15-minute interval
volumes that summed to produce the highest volume at the intersection were then determined.

The estimated existing 2009 traffic volumes are made up of the traffic volumes that were
collected in 2005 and a growth of approximately 2.0% in the area. The volumes were increased
for four years to represent the volumes that are on the roadway in 2009. The existing 2009 peak
hour volumes are shown in Figure 2 for morning and evening peak hours. Existing traffic

Shopping Center. Since the development includes retail component, pass-by reductions has been
applied. Mixed-use reductions can also be applicable since proposed development includes retail

applied in trip generation to evaluate future traffic operations. The calculated trip generation for

development is expected to be completed in 2011, so volumes were increased for two more years
to represent the volumes that will be on the roadway in the future, in addition to any traffic from
the proposed development.

CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Traffic impacts were evaluated due to the proposed Mixed-Use Development along SR 9
(Roswell Road) to the northwest of the intersection of SR 9 (Roswell Road) / Hammond Drive in
Sandy Springs, Georgia. The development is proposed to consist of 6,409 s.f. of retail space and
a 273 unit mid-rise apartment. Existing and future traffic operations at the intersections of SR 9
(Roswell Road) / Hammond Drive, SR 9 (Roswell Road) / Sandy Springs Place, Sandy Springs
Place / Kroger Backside Driveway have been evaluated. In addition, the proposed site driveways
on Sandy Springs Place have been evaluated for the future condition.

The results of the traffic operations analysis revealed that the existing intersections of SR 9
(Roswell Road) / Sandy Springs Place, Sandy Springs Place / Kroger Backside Driveway which
are operating at an acceptable level of service, will continue to operate at an acceptable LOS
during both the AM and PM peak hours after the addition of site generated traffic from the
proposed development, if all recommended improvements are implemented.

At the intersection of SR 9 (Roswell Road) / Hammond Drive, it is recommended that a
dedicated southbound right turn lane be added on SR 9. In addition, traffic signal equipment at
this intersection should be upgraded to include southbound right turn lane.

At the intersection of SR 9 (Roswell Road) / Sandy Springs Place, it is recommended that an
eastbound right turn lane be added on Sandy Spring Place. The signal and pedestrian facilities
should be upgraded to include this right turn lane.

At the proposed Site Driveways on Sandy Springs Place, it is recommended that the intersections
have stop controlled side streets (Retail Driveway and Residential Driveways 1 & 2), with Sandy
Springs Place remaining free flow. Shared left / right turn lanes are recommended on the Sandy
Springs Retail Driveway and Residential Driveways 1 & 2 northbound approach for traffic
exiting the development.

In addition, a queuing analysis has been done at the intersections of SR 9 / Sandy Springs Place
and Sandy Spring Place / Sandy Springs Retail Driveway. The 95th percentile queue length
analysis shows that the westbound left turn queues at the intersection of Sandy Spring Place /
Sandy Springs Retail Driveway are significantly less and hence it will not interfere with the
eastbound left turning queues at the intersection of SR 9 / Sandy Springs Place.
