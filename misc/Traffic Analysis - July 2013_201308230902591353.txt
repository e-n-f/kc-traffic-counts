Greenman Pedersen, Inc. (GPI) has conducted a traffic assessment to evaluate the potential
impacts associated with providing van rental services through the Home Depot Tool Rental
Center at 615 Arsenal Street in Watertown, Massachusetts (Project). The Project includes
designating 16 existing parking spaces for use by Home Depot for the parking of rental vans.
This evaluation includes a trip generation assessment as well as a capacity analysis of the
Arsenal Street at Arlington Street/Coolidge Street/Arsenal Mall Driveway intersection.

Based on standard Institute of Transportation Engineers' (ITE) data the peak times for home-
improvement superstores generally occur between 12 noon and 4 PM on a typical weekday and
Saturday. Accordingly, GPI has collected new peak hour traffic volumes at the study area
intersection on Saturday, July 13, 2013 between 11 AM and 2 PM and on Tuesday July 16, 2013
between 4 PM and 6 PM. While these periods do not explicitly align with the peak times of the
Home Depot, for the purposes of providing a conservative estimate of traffic impacts it is
assumed that the peak hour traffic generated by the van rental service aligns with the peak hour
of adjacent street traffic. All traffic count information is provided as an attachment to this
document.

 This memorandum supplements GPI's July 1, 2013 parking evaluation memorandum which
examined the potential parking impacts associated with designating 16 existing parking spaces
for use by this new service.

TRIP GENERATION

To provide a conservative trip generation estimate for the proposed van rental service it is
assumed that each of the maximum of 8 proposed rental vehicles to be provided on site at the
beginning of any given day are to be rented and returned during the same day. It is further
assumed that each rentee is driven to the site by another party, who then drops off the rentee and
leaves the site. This party then returns in a separate vehicle upon returning the rental van to
drive the rentee off site at the end of the day. A summary of the trips associated with this
activity is below:

           1 vehicle with 2 parties (rentee plus other) enter the site to initiate van rental ­ 1 trip
           The rental van plus original vehicle exit the site upon initiating rental ­ 2 trips
           The rental van, plus an additional vehicle return to site to return rental vehicle ­ 2 trips
           1 vehicle exits the site (with rentee plus other) upon returning rental vehicle ­ 1 trip

           Sub- Total = 6 tips/rental vehicle

           Assume all 8 rental vehicles are rented and returned in one day = 48 trips

To provide an additional layer of conservatism it is further assumed that an additional 2 van
rentals are returned to the site over the course of the day in a manner similar to that described
above. To ensure that a maximum 8 vehicles are maintained on the site at any given time these 2
vans would then be transported off-site to another home depot site in need of vehicles.

           The rental van, plus an additional vehicle return to site to return rental vehicle ­ 2 trips
           1 vehicle exits the site (with rentee plus other) upon returning rental vehicle ­ 1 trip
           The rental van plus additional vehicle exit the site to remove van from site ­ 2 trips
           1 vehicle returns to the site upon reallocating van to different site ­ 1 trip

           Sub-Total = 6 trips/rental vehicle

           Assume 2 vans to be returned and reallocated to different site = 12 trips

           Grand Total = 60 trips/day

Therefore, for the purposes of this traffic assessment it was assumed that the proposed van rental
service would generate 60 trips per day, 30 in and 30 out. Assuming that all rental pick-ups and
the rental drop-offs occur within two 3-hour windows (say 7 AM to 10 AM and 4 PM to 7 PM),
this will generate 10 trips per hour (5 in and 5 out), or one additional trip every six minutes. It
should be noted that while these trips were assumed to occur during peak traffic periods for the

CAPACITY ANALYSIS ME THODOLOGY

A primary result of capacity analysis is the assignment of levels of service to traffic facilities un-
der various traffic flow conditions. The capacity analysis methodology is based on the concepts
and procedures in the Highway Capacity Manual (HCM).1 The concept of level of service (LOS)
is defined as a qualitative measure describing operational conditions within a traffic stream and
their perception by motorists and/or passengers. A level-of-service definition provides an index
to quality of traffic flow in terms of such factors as speed, travel time, freedom to maneuver, traf-
fic interruptions, comfort, convenience, and safety.

Six levels of service are defined for each type of facility. They are given letter designations from
A to F, with LOS A representing the best operating conditions and LOS F the worst. Since the
level of service of a traffic facility is a function of the traffic flows placed upon it, such a facility
may operate at a wide range of levels of service, depending on the time of day, day of week, or
period of year. A description of the operating condition under each level of service is provided
below:

           LOS A describes conditions with little to no delay to motorists.
           LOS B represents a desirable level with relatively low delay to motorists.

           LOS C describes conditions with average delays to motorists.
           LOS D describes operations where the influence of congestion becomes more noticeable.
           Delays are still within an acceptable range.
           LOS E represents operating conditions with high delay values. This level is considered by
           many agencies to be the limit of acceptable delay.
           LOS F is considered to be unacceptable to most drivers with high delay values that often
           occur, when arrival flow rates exceed the capacity of the intersection.

Signalized Intersections

Levels of service for signalized intersections are calculated using the operational analysis meth-
odology of the HCM. The methodology for signalized intersections assesses the effects of signal
type, timing, phasing, and progression; vehicle mix; and geometrics on average control delay.
Control delay includes initial deceleration delay, queue move-up time, stopped delay, and final
acceleration delay. Table 2 summarizes the relationship between level of service and average
control delay.

CONCLUSION
The proposed project involves incorporating van rental service through the Home Depot Tool
Rental Center at 615 Arsenal Street in Watertown, Massachusetts. It is conservatively estimated
that this service would generate up to 60 daily vehicle trips. While it is anticipated that the
majority of these trips would occur during non peak hour traffic conditions to avoid congestion,
to be conservative it was assumed that peak periods associated with the van rental service
coincides with peak traffic conditions. Nevertheless, the weekday PM and Saturday midday
peak period capacity analyses indicate that any impact from the additional 10 peak hour trips
associated with the van rental service through the Arsenal Street at Arlington Street/Coolidge
Street/Arsenal Mall Driveway intersection would be negligible, with no change to level of
service anticipated. It is therefore concluded that this intersection could accommodate the traffic
associated with the proposed van rental service without any further modifications.
