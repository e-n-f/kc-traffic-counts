                           EXECUTIVE SUMMARY:
Comprehensive Engineering Services, Inc. completed a Roundabout Justification Study for the
intersection of CR 455 at CR 561 in Lake County. This rural intersection of two, two-lane
roadways has been the scene of multiple angle collisions which may be mitigated by the
installation of a roundabout. Current intersection control consists of stop-control imposed on the
CR 455 approaches only (free-flow along CR 561). The three year crash analysis period of
2005-2007 generated a crash rate of 1.26 crashes per million-entering-vehicles and one fatal
angle crash was also reported at this location.

This study was based on the Roundabout Justification Study procedures presented in the
FDOT's Manual on Uniform Traffic Studies, supplemented with a benefit-cost assessment of a
conceptual roundabout design to address any safety justification for this type of intersection
control. Although alternative design modifications could be incorporated in the near term to
mitigate some of the crashes at the intersection, the development and presentation of these
design alternatives was excluded at the request of Lake County.

Based on the existing weekday peak period traffic volumes, a roundabout is anticipated to
operate with a Level of Service (LOS) A. This level of operation is slightly better than that
expected from a simple signal installation (LOS A/B), all-way stop-control (LOS B), or the
existing two-way stop control (LOS A/B). From a safety perspective, the existing intersection
configuration generates 60 conflict points and a roundabout would produce only eight points of
conflict. Considering this, a benefit-cost ratio for a conceptual 180 foot diameter roundabout
yielded a value of 7.0. Based upon the capacity and safety improvements and considering
the local development and the operating and geometric characteristics of the
intersecting roadways, a roundabout is considered justified for the intersection of CR
455 and CR 561.

1.      PURPOSE & SCOPE:
     The purpose of this study is to conduct a roundabout justification evaluation at the
     intersection of CR 561 at CR 455 in Lake County, Florida. For safety reasons resulting from
     the current geometric complexity, Lake County requested that a roundabout be evaluated at
     this location to determine safety, operational and cost impacts. Also at the request of Lake
     County, this study scope is limited only to evaluating the roundabout as a long-term potential
     improvement. The development and presentation of any potential short-term (interim) safety
     or operational improvements have therefore been excluded.

     This study was conducted at the request of the Florida Department of Transportation
     (FDOT). The general procedure used to evaluate the potential roundabout was derived
     from the Department's Roundabout Justification Study procedures (FDOT Form #750-020-
     18). A benefit-cost ratio was also developed based on anticipated mitigated crashes from
     incorporation of a roundabout at this location.

3.      CRASH HISTORY:
     A major contributing factor to the consideration of an alternative intersection design at this
     location was recent vehicular crashes as noted by Lake County. Crash data was therefore
     provided by Lake County to the FDOT which was supplemented with additional crash data
     from FDOT and provided for documentation and analysis purposes.

     A total of 41 crash reports were reviewed for the period of 2005 to 2008, however the 2008
     crash data was considered under-reported at the time the crash information was obtained.
     Of the 41 crash reports reviewed, 14 were determined to be related to the subject
     intersection. A brief summary of the annual crash data is included as Table 4 and a detailed

     Figure 4 on the following page presents a crash plot graphically depicting the crash types,
     vehicular directions and injury severities.

     During the analysis period, one fatal crash occurred at the subject intersection. On
     Thursday, September 29, 2005 at 5:45 PM, the driver of a westbound vehicle failed to stop
     for the stop sign along CR 455 and entered the intersection with CR 561. The westbound
     vehicle struck a northbound vehicle which was pushed into the path of a southbound
     vehicle. Upon striking the northbound vehicle, the driver of the southbound vehicle was
     killed in the crash. The crash occurred under wet pavement conditions and tests performed
     on the driver of the westbound vehicle later revealed that he was driving under the influence
     (cocaine) at the time of the crash.

     Considering the estimated entering vehicles presented as Table 3, the 2005-2007 average
     historic crash rate for this intersection is 1.26 crashes per million entering vehicles.

4.       CONCEPTUAL ROUNDABOUT:
     In order to evaluate the safety, operational and construction cost impacts associated with
     the installation of a roundabout at the subject location, a conceptual roundabout was
     designed. The conceptual design is presented at the end of this section as Figure 5 and
     was based on design considerations presented in Federal Highway Administration's
     "Roundabouts: An Informational Guide" (FHWA). It should be noted that this conceptual
     design was developed solely for general operational, safety and cost estimating purposes
     and is not intended to represent a final roundabout design. Formal design of the roundabout
     will require the collection of vertical and horizontal survey data and will need to include
     review and approval by the local maintaining agency and the FDOT.

     As the proposed roundabout location is at the intersection of two, two-lane rural roadways
     with few pedestrians and high approach speeds, the proposed roundabout should be a rural
     single-lane type. Based on this classification, the design entry speed should be 25 MPH
     and the inscribed circle diameter is typically 115 to 130 feet. Through coordination with the
     Department and field observations, large semi-trailer vehicles often traverse the subject
     intersection. Based on this condition, the single-lane conceptual roundabout design
     considered a WB-67 design vehicle, and a conservative 180 foot inscribed circle diameter
     was used. The formal design of the roundabout will require careful consideration and
     possible additional design modifications in order to control vehicle entry speeds. The
     incorporation of larger design radii for large trucks can also facilitate high-speed entries by
     passenger vehicles. A significant difference between entry speeds and circulating speeds
     by various vehicles can lead to operational and safety conflicts. This design issue will be a
     notable factor to be addressed as part of the formal design.

     In addition to the 180 foot inscribed circle diameter for the conceptual roundabout, the
     following design parameters were considered for the single-lane roundabout and
     subsequent cost estimate presented later in this study. One or more of these factors may
     need to be modified as part of the formal design effort:
           20 foot wide circulating travel lane bordered by a 13 foot wide traversable truck
             apron
           114 foot diameter raised (curbed) central island
           360 foot long painted splitter islands (200 foot raised) on each approach
           Striped approach and departure lane widths of approximately 14 feet at the entrance
             and exit areas from the roundabout
           Assumed eight light fixtures with a $10,000 installation cost and $300 annual
             maintenance cost each to encompass the roundabout and the approaches
           Assumed three feet of fill throughout footprint of roundabout to flatten profile grades
             along the approaches, improve visibility of the roundabout and alleviate drainage
             concerns
           Reduce speed limits along the approaches to the roundabout via signing and rumble
             strips in order to prepare drivers for a 25 MPH entry / operational speed

As previously noted, there are eight utility poles located in the vicinity of the roundabout with
four located immediately adjacent to the central intersection. Based on information obtained
from the Department, these poles have been installed via permit, therefore relocation and
associated costs will be the responsibility of utility agency owner(s).

A critical issue to the design of this roundabout will be the reduction of the approach speeds.
The current posted speed limits to the existing intersection are 55 MPH northbound and 50
MPH southbound along the free-flow CR 561 approaches. The stop-controlled approaches
along CR 455 currently have posted approach speed limits of 55 MPH eastbound and 45
MPH westbound. The proposed roundabout is anticipated to impose yield-controlled entries
with a posted circulating advisory speed limit of 25 MPH. At 45 MPH, the stopping sight
distance (45 ­ 0 MPH) is approximately 360 feet. As approximately 360 feet of sight
distance to the roundabout should be available along the southbound, eastbound and
westbound approaches, reduction of the posted speed limits to this speed is assumed prior
to the vicinity of the roundabout. Since the posted speed limit is currently 45 MPH along the
westbound approach, no advanced speed reductions are planned for this leg. The
northbound CR 561 intersection approach is located just beyond a horizontal curve with a
posted advisory speed of 35 MPH. To reinforce this speed and to better correlate available
stopping sight distance to field conditions, it is recommended that the posted speed for the
northbound CR 561 approach be reduced to 35 MPH just prior to this sight-restricting
horizontal curve. As the current posted approach speed limit is 55 MPH, two sets of
"Reduced Speed Ahead / XX MPH" assemblies are anticipated.

An additional device which was considered to reinforce the advanced warning signage for
the roundabout is the installation of rumble strips along the approaches. Currently, the
westbound and eastbound stop-controlled approaches include rumble strips however it is
recommended that all approaches be retrofitted with rumble strips as part of the roundabout
construction effort.

5.       OPERATIONAL ANALYSIS:
     Critical weekday operational periods were derived from the turning movement counts
     collected for this location. The morning and evening peak hours were selected as critical
     operational hours as the total intersection entering volumes during these periods were 785
     and 793, respectively. In comparison, the midday total entering volume peaked at 500
     vehicles-per-hour or approximately 1/3 less than the morning and evening peaks.

     The operational evaluation of the intersection was derived from the FDOT's Manual on
     Uniform Traffic Studies, specifically the Roundabout Justification Study procedure (FDOT
     Form #750-020-18). A copy of the completed study form with all supporting data is included
     as Appendix C.

     From an operational standpoint, the existing 2010 operations are adequate with minimal
     overall intersection delay. The existing operational conditions for the five effective
     intersections at this location were modeled using Synchro software and the approach and
     overall intersection levels of service were estimated as follows:

      Midday          B            B             A        A                  2.1"          A
      PM Peak         C            C             A        A                  2.9"          A

     An operational-related item observed in the field was the limited use of the by-pass lanes by
     turning vehicles at the intersection. Several vehicles were found to turn at the central
     intersection versus using the outer by-pass lanes and this was more evident with large
     vehicles. Over the 8-hour turning movement count period, 56% of all turning trucks utilized
     the by-pass lanes while 73% of all turning passenger vehicles utilized the by-pass lanes.
     This combined with rutting and evidence of off-tracking along the by-pass lanes suggests
     that many drivers (especially those with larger vehicles), are apprehensive about using the
     8-9 foot wide travel lanes along these roadway segments.

     Further investigation of the by-pass lane operations revealed that as volumes increased on
     the by-pass lanes, more left-turning vehicles (trucks and passenger vehicles) utilized the
     central intersection. As a result, the higher-volume by-pass lanes essentially became de-
     facto channelized right turn lanes. The supporting data follows as Table 6 and is based on
     the eight hour weekday total turning movement count data collected at the subject
     intersection. Overall, 98% of right-turning vehicles utilized their respective by-pass lanes
     while only 43% of left-turning vehicles utilized their respective by-pass lanes.

6.       BENEFIT-COST ANALYSIS:
     As a supplement to completing the Roundabout Justification Study procedure (Appendix C),
     a benefit-cost ratio assessment was completed for the installation of a roundabout at the
     subject intersection. Estimated construction costs and crash mitigations related to the
     roundabout are included as Appendix D.

     The benefit value is defined as the value of annual mitigated crashes anticipated from the
     proposed improvements. Due to the operational nature of a roundabout, more severe
     crashes such as angle, left turn and head-on collisions were anticipated to incur the highest
     level of mitigation. Crash reduction factors for these crashes were conservatively assumed
     as 80% for head-on collisions and 50% for angle and left turn collisions, with reduced levels
     of mitigation for those crashes involving a driver under the influence. Raw cost-per-crash
     information was provided by the Department and represents average 2006-2008 Historic
     Crash Cost Values for FDOT State Roadways in District Five. The cost-per-crash value
     used for a two-lane rural undivided facility is $409,704.

     The construction cost for roundabout installation was based on the conceptual roundabout
     design presented in the previous section. A formal design effort will require several
     elements which may affect constructability and cost. These factors are beyond the scope of
     this study effort but will include utility coordination, drainage design, formal roadway lighting
     design and needs, formal pavement design, a community awareness effort, local
     government coordination, landscaping needs and an extensive maintenance of traffic effort.

     Based on both the conceptual roundabout design included as Figure 5 and the anticipated
     associated crash mitigations for a recent three year crash history, the estimated benefit-
     cost ratio for installation of a roundabout is 7.0. Further details of this evaluation are

7.      CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS:
The purpose of this study was to evaluate the potential installation of a roundabout at the
intersection of CR 455 at CR 561 in Lake County to address existing safety concerns. Although
alternative design modifications could be incorporated in the near term to mitigate some of the
crashes at the intersection, the development and presentation of these design alternatives was
excluded at the request of Lake County.

The location for the potential roundabout is at the intersection of CR 455 at CR 561 in Lake
County. Both of these roads are rural two-lane facilities and the CR 455 approaches to the
intersection are currently stop-controlled (free-flow along CR 561).        The Roundabout
Justification Study procedures presented in the FDOT's Manual of Uniform Traffic Studies was
used as the foundation for the assessment. This procedure considers operations, safety and
several other physical or geometric conditions which may impact operations or constructability
of a roundabout.

From an operational standpoint, the roundabout option is anticipated to generate delays similar
but slightly lower than those generated from a basic signal installation, all-way stop control, or
the existing two-way stop control. An evaluation of weekday traffic conditions revealed that
neither a signal nor all-way stop control is warranted at this intersection at this time.

From a capacity standpoint, roundabouts can handle a total entering volume of approximately
2,000 vehicles-per-hour. The existing peak weekday hour entering volume to the intersection
totals 793 vehicles, or a volume-to-capacity ratio of 0.4. Furthermore, traffic volumes in the
vicinity of the intersection have been decreasing since 2005 at a rate of nearly 5% annually.

The Roundabout Justification Study procedure also presents the need to consider
contraindicting factors to the installation of a roundabout. There are no characteristics of the
intersection or vicinity which would appear to negatively affect the safety or operations of a
roundabout under typical weekday conditions. Furthermore, the amount of available right-of-
way appears to be adequate based upon the footprint of the existing intersection design.

The existing intersection configuration includes both a central intersection as well as four
surrounding intersections with limited access. Overall, this geometry generates 60 conflict
points. A roundabout at this location would reduce this number of conflict points to only eight.
Based on this assessment, a crash reduction estimate was derived for roundabout installation.
Utilizing conservative estimates, a total of 6.1 of the reported 13 crashes at this location (2005-
2007) are anticipated to be mitigated. A benefit-cost ratio for this level of crash reduction
yielded a value of 7.0.

From an operational (capacity), and safety (benefit-cost ratio) standpoint, the installation of a
roundabout at the intersection of CR 455 and CR 561 would appear to be an acceptable
alternate traffic control option. The installation of a roundabout is therefore considered to be
justified.
