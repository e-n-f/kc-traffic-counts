EXISTING AND PROPOSED CONDITIONS
Parker Road is State Highway 83 and the State Highway Access Code governs access. In
addition, the SH-83/SH-86 Access Control Plan defines the type and location of access
under an agreement with CDOT, counties, and communities within the corridor. Parker
Road is a six-lane principal arterial that currently carries 40,000 AADT according to
CDOT traffic data. The posted speed limit is 55 mph. The Code defines it as an NR-A
highway. CDOT's 20-year growth factor is 1.32 on this section of the highway. A 30-foot
grassy median divides the highway. The Parker Road/Stroh Road intersection is under
traffic signal control. Presently there are no plans to improve either road.

ACCESS LOCATIONS
Two accesses are proposed. From Parker Road at the northern end a 3/4 movement
access will provide a left in (no left out) from SB Parker Road and a right in/right out on
the NB lanes. On Stroh Road approximately 500 feet to the east of Parker Road, there
will be a full-movement access.

TRAFFIC IMPACT
ATC uses Synchro v.7 for operations analyses. The Synchro v.7 methodology is based on
the Highway Capacity Manual 2000 (HCM). The Synchro HCM reports are attached for
reference. The chart summarizes the forecast 2014 and 2035 LOS (level of service). LOS
is letter rating from A to F. LOS A indicates free-flow traffic conditions and no delay at
intersections. LOS F is heavy traffic congestion with significant delay. LOS is provided
for the overall operations at signalized intersections. LOS D is generally the benchmark
for acceptable signalized intersection operations during the weekday peak hours. The
critical movement, not the overall, provides the LOS rating for unsignalized intersections.
The critical movement is generally a left turn from the minor approach. Caution is
advised when evaluating the LOS at unsignalized intersections particularly when LOS F
shows. In cases of an LOS F, the HCM1 suggests that other evaluation measures should
be considered such as the volume over capacity ratio and 95th percentile queue length to
make the most effective traffic control decision. LOS F at unsignalized intersections is
often normal for the weekday peak hour when the v/c ratio and the 95th percentile queue
length are acceptable. The following chart summarizes the AM/PM LOS for the overall
signalized and the critical movement at the unsignalized accesses.

SYSTEM ANALYSIS
The LOS E at the intersection in the AM peak hour improves to D by adding a left turn
lane on the WB approach and changing the left turn phasing from split to protected. At all
other times the intersection LOS is acceptable at D or better. The left turn in at the 3/4
movement will operate acceptably in all conditions. The LOS E in the 2032 AM is
because of the control delay of 38.7 seconds. However, the 95th percentile queue length is
72 feet (three vehicles) and the v/c ratio is .54. Both of these performance measures are
acceptable. All movements at the full movement access function acceptably in all of the
analysis periods.

CONCLUSION & RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the analysis, traffic generated by Reata Ridge Village can be easily absorbed
and will not cause a safety or operational problem on the adjacent streets and
intersections. The proposed access locations are the best engineering fit for the parcel's
configuration and for matching to the internal street layout.

Regarding the 3/4 movement access on Parker Road, this was thoroughly examined in a
separate technical memorandum. It concluded that left in movement would function well
and without adverse impact to the northbound traffic flow. A deceleration lane of 800
feet that includes an 18.5:1 taper is required and can be constructed within the existing
grassy median. The technical memorandum is attached for reference.

At the full-movement access, left and right-turn deceleration lanes are recommended.
Stroh Road is posted at 40 mph and would be considered an NR-B by access code
standards. The town defers to the access code standards for accel and decel lanes. In this
case, the deceleration lanes should be approximately 250 feet including the taper of 12:1.
This would provide about 100 feet of storage.

No improvements to Parker or Stroh intersection are necessitated by this project's traffic
impact. However, in the future improvements to the westbound approach are
recommended to improve the LOS from E to D. The improvements would include a left
turn storage lane and changing the split phase to protected.

                                  Technical Memorandum
                              Proposed 3/4 Movement Access
                            Parker Road at Reata Ridge Village1

Purpose
This technical memorandum analyzes the feasibility of constructing a 3/4 movement
access on Parker Road approximately 825 feet north of Stroh Road. The access will
provide service to Reata Ridge Village, a planned mixed-use development of 105 single-
family homes and 7 commercial/retail lots for uses such as a gas station, fast-food
restaurants, bank, and medical offices. Presently, there is an existing right in/right out to
the property on Parker Road located approximately 125 south of the proposed. The
village plan shows a full-movement access on Stroh Road approximately 500 feet east of
Parker Road. There are no other access locations. The aerial photo shows the location of
the property and accesses.
                                       Aldridge Transportation Consultants, LLC
                                                                                 Page 2 of 6


A 3/4 movement access would allow Reata Ridge Village patrons and residents
convenient access from SB Parker Road. This type of access is commonplace even on
relatively high-speed arterial highways. The State Highway Access Code allows for 3/4
movement accesses on NR-A highways and most the state's access control plans include
them as a reasonably available option.

This technical memorandum will analyze in detail the operations, safety, sight distance,
and geometric design. The operations analysis includes a determination of the critical gap
and gap acceptance. In addition, the study discusses the Code and provisions of the SH-
83/86 Access Control Plan.

Overview
Parker Road is State Highway 83 and the State Highway Access Code governs access. In
addition, the ACP defines the type and location of access under an agreement with
CDOT, counties, and communities within the corridor. Parker Road is a six-lane principal
arterial that currently carries 40,000 AADT according to CDOT traffic data. The posted
speed limit is 55 mph. The Code defines it as an NR-A highway. CDOT's 20-year growth
factor is 1.32 on this section of the highway. A 30-foot grassy median divides the
highway. The Parker Road/Stroh Road intersection is under traffic signal control.

State Highway Access Code
Under Section 3.10.5, left turns in (3/4 movement) may be allowed if the addition will
improve operations at an adjacent full-movement intersection, and meet appropriate
design criteria, and significant operational or safety problems would not occur. The
movement should also be beneficial to the general public, particularly when the location
of the access requires reconstruction of a non-traversable median.

The design requirement for a left turn deceleration lane on an NR-A highway at 55 mph
is 600 feet in length including taper (18.5:1) plus additional length for storage, which in
this case is 200 feet. The design requirements can be satisfied without significant
renovations to the highway.

SH-83/86 Access Control Plan
The ACP references this access in item number 72. Specifically it states, "This private
access currently functions as a right in/right out intersection. Revise access if property
develops, or if safety or operations issues occur." Given that the description indicates that
a revision is possible when the property develops, it seems reasonable that an amendment
to the ACP is not required. Nevertheless, that decision is up to the town and CDOT.

Operations
As mentioned earlier, 3/4 movement intersections are relatively commonplace throughout
the metro area even on high-volume, high-speed highways. For instance, on Santa Fe at
Rio Grande, there is a 3/4 movement that requires crossing four lanes running at 55 mph,
including an accelerating on-ramp movement from Belleview. On US-85 at 144th Ave.,
CDOT recently installed one and approved another at Denver St. in Brighton. CCD and
CDOT approved maintaining a 3/4 movement on Hampden Blvd. just east of I-25 after it
was proven that the access operated safely. It is also a fact that left turns on a permitted
green at signalized intersections function in a similar fashion to an unsignalized 3/4
movement intersection. On Parker Road, within the vicinity of Stroh Road, there are two

intersections with permitted/protected left turn phasing on the mainline approaches.
There is one at J. Morgan Road and another north of Hess Road at Pine Drive.

July 18, 2012, All Traffic Data, a professional traffic-counting firm, took AM and PM
peak hour traffic counts at the intersection of Stroh Road. In addition, ATD performed a
gap study on the NB direction at the location of the proposed 3/4 movement intersection.
The study revealed the number of two-second gaps per hour within a 24-hour period. The
appendix contains the traffic count and gap study results. The graphic below shows the
gap study.




To determine if the number of available gaps is sufficient for a safe crossing, the time in
seconds to move across three through lanes and a right-turn deceleration lane,
approximately 50 feet, calculates from acceleration rates for normal and maximum
acceleration of a passenger car starting at zero. The formula is D= 1/2(3.6 feet per secs^2)
for normal acceleration and D= 1/2(7.5 feet per secs^2) for maximum acceleration. This
means that to traverse 50 feet at normal acceleration takes 5.25 seconds and at maximum
acceleration, 3.65 seconds. It is reasonable to assume that acceleration at these types of
intersection is slightly quicker than normal. Therefore, based on the rate of acceleration a
gap of between 5 and 6 seconds is a rational starting point.

The Highway Capacity Manual takes a different approach and factors in adjustments for
heavy vehicles, grade, and intersection/roadway geometry. The critical gap is 4.1 seconds
according to the HCM for a left turn from a major street. The HCM assumes the base
                                       Aldridge Transportation Consultants, LLC

factors for a six-lane facility are the same as those for a four-lane major street. The
follow-up time is 2.2 seconds ­ the time between the departures of one vehicle to the
departure of the next vehicle using the same major street gap.

During the AM peak hour, there are 97 gaps of 5 to 10 seconds sufficient for 97 vehicle
crossings, 50 gaps of 11 to 18 seconds sufficient for 100 vehicle crossings, 20 gaps of 19
to 28 seconds sufficient for 60 vehicle crossings and 6 gaps of greater than 29 seconds for
24 or more vehicle crossings. In all, an estimated 281 vehicle crossings could occur
during the AM peak hour. Using the HCM methodology, theoretically, 719 vehicles
could make a safe crossing assuming a continuous queue and maximum number of
follow-up vehicles per gap.

the PM peak hour. Using the HCM methodology, theoretically, 775 vehicles could make
a safe crossing assuming a continuous queue and maximum number of follow-up vehicles

Safety
As often the case, the perception that something is unsafe frequently overrules reality.
Unfortunately, it can be quite difficult to change someone's perception. Recently, both
CDOT and CCD traffic engineers wanted to close a 3/4 movement access on Hampden
Ave. just east of I-25 believing to be unsafe. ATC prepared an extensive traffic accident
report and found that only one accident in four years was attributable to a left turn
crossing movement. Because of the accident analysis CDOT and CCD agreed to leave it
open. The vast majority of accidents are random occurrences and therefore unpredictable.
Assuming that the intersection meets design standards including sight distance then the
intersection is safe according to the CDOT Design Guide. Unless an accident pattern
emerges (three or more accidents within a year of the same type) correctable by a
movement prohibition or a traffic control device, the general assumption also has to be
that the movement is safe.

With regard to sight distance, 550 feet is the design standard at 55-mph. The roadway is
flat and there are encumbrances to the 550-foot standard.

Conclusions and Recommendations
This analysis finds that the 3/4 movement intersection meets the generally accepted
criteria promulgated by the Code and the ACP and therefore should be allowed. It will
benefit the Reata Ridge Village by providing a convenient and safe entrance for traffic on
SB Parker Road. It is a commonplace design found throughout the metro area frequently
on high-volume and high-speed roadways. The left turn movement across one direction
of traffic occurs at intersections with permitted/protected left turn phasing on mainline
approaches.

Operationally, this analysis based on actual data finds that there are sufficient gaps in the
NB traffic flow to permit at least 281/326 (practical) and up to 719/775 (theoretical)
opportunities in the traffic flow for a safe crossing in the AM/PM peak hours,
respectively. In addition, the operations analysis shows that it will benefit the operation
of the Stroh intersection by improving the AM/PM peak hours LOS from D/D to C/C.

Finally, the 3/4 movement intersection should be considered safe as there would be no
design issues with its construction. The Code design standards are achievable and the
sight distance for crossing traffic is adequate.
