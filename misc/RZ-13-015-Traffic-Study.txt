1.0     INTRODUCTION

This report presents the analysis of the anticipated traffic impacts associated with the proposed
McGinnis Ferry Road Tract, a mixed-use development consisting of approximately 116 single family
homes, 124 townhomes, and 85,000 square-feet of retail/office space. The approximate 51-acre site is
located on the south side of McGinnis Ferry Road just east of Bell Road in the City of Johns Creek,
Georgia. The proposed development will be served by two driveways along McGinnis Ferry Road and
one driveway along Bell Road. Figure 1 and Figure 2 provide a location map and aerial photograph of
the proposed site. Additionally, a copy of the proposed site plan is provided in Appendix A. The
development is scheduled to be completed by 2017, and this analysis will consider the existing 2013
traffic conditions, the projected 2017 no-build traffic conditions (background traffic growth), and the
projected 2017 build conditions (background traffic growth plus the proposed development traffic).

2.0     STUDY AREA DETERMINATION

The study area has been identified to include the proposed site access as well as two existing signalized
intersections along McGinnis Ferry Road: the intersection at Bell Road / Old Atlanta Road (Intersection
#1) and the intersection at Blackstone Way (Intersection #2). The proposed site access will consist of
three driveways: one full-movement driveway on McGinnis Ferry Road at the existing Shopping Center
Driveway intersection, one right-in/right-out driveway farther to the east along McGinnis Ferry Road
(west of Blackstone Way), and one full-movement driveway along Bell Road. For the purposes of the
traffic impact study, McGinnis Ferry Road is considered to have an east-west orientation, and both Bell
Road and Blackstone Way are considered to have a north-south orientation.

3.0         EXISTING TRAFFIC CONDITIONS

day and a posted speed limit of 45 MPH. Bell Road is a 2-lane urban collector with a 2012 AADT of
5460 vehicles per day and a posted speed limit of 45 MPH. Blackstone Way is a 2-lane urban local road
with a posted speed limit of 25 MPH. Vehicle peak hour turning movement counts were performed at
the two signalized intersections in the study area as well as the unsignalized intersection at Shopping

5.0      PROJECT TRAFFIC

Project traffic used in this analysis is defined as the vehicle trips expected to be generated by the
development and the distribution and assignment of that traffic over the study roadway network. This
traffic impact study evaluated the impacts of developing 116 single family homes, 124 townhomes, and
85,000 square-feet of retail/office. While it is uncertain how the 85,000 square-feet will be developed,
for the purposes of this traffic study, it was assumed to be 29,000 square-feet of office and 56,000
square-feet of retail.

Access to the proposed McGinnis Ferry Road Tract mixed-use development will be provided at three (3)

proposed access point follows:

      1. Site Driveway #1 (Intersection #3) ­ a proposed full-movement driveway at an existing median
         opening along McGinnis Ferry Road at Shopping Center Driveway.

Not all of the trips generated by this development will be new external trips. According to the internal
capture methodologies presented in the Institute of Transportation Engineers' (ITE) Trip Generation
Handbook, Ninth Edition, 2012, this development is expected to have an overall internal capture rate of
15.8% throughout the day and 15.5% during the PM peak hour. Applying this internal capture rate
accounts for the trips among the various land uses that are internal to the site rather than using the major
street system.

Many retail developments attract pass-by trips, which are trips that are already on the adjacent roadway
network. According to the Institute of Transportation Engineers' (ITE) Trip Generation Handbook,
Ninth Edition, 2012, the average PM peak period pass-by trip percentage for retail/commercial of this
size is 46.2%. This pass-by capture rate was applied at the three proposed access points to the site.

5.3     Trip Distribution and Assignment

The directional distribution and assignment of new project trips was based on the percentages used in
the previous traffic study, a review of land uses and population densities in the area, and the existing
peak hour turning movement counts. For residential traffic, 15% was assigned to the north, 25% to the
south, 35% to the east, and 25% to the west. For office and retail, 10% was assigned to the north, 20% to
the south, 50% to the east, and 20% to the west.

Based on the trip generation from Table 1 and the anticipated trip distribution, new project trips were
assigned to the study roadway network. Figure 5 illustrates the projected 2017 build-out traffic
conditions, including new intersection laneage, for the AM and PM peak hours. Appendix D provides

6.0     LEVEL-OF-SERVICE ANALYSIS

Level-of-service determinations were made for the weekday AM and PM peak hours for the existing
study network intersections and proposed access intersections using Synchro Professional, Version 8.0.
The program uses methodologies contained in the 2000 Highway Capacity Manual to determine the
operating characteristics of an intersection. Capacity is defined as the maximum number of vehicles that
can pass over a particular road segment or through a particular intersection within a specified period
under prevailing roadway, traffic, and control conditions.

Level-of-service (LOS) is used to describe the operating characteristics of a road segment or intersection
in relation to its capacity. LOS is defined as a qualitative measure that describes operational conditions
and motorists' perceptions of a traffic stream. The Highway Capacity Manual defines six levels of
service, LOS A through LOS F, with A being the best and F the worst.

For the purposes of this traffic impact study, a level-of-service standard of D was assumed for all
intersections within the study network. This assumption is consistent with local and state agency review
standards for the study area.

Levels-of-service for unsignalized intersections, with stop control on the minor street(s) only, are
reported for the side street approaches. Low levels-of-service for the side street approaches are not
uncommon, as vehicles may experience a delay turning onto a major roadway.

Levels-of-service for signalized intersections are reported for the intersection as a whole. One or more
movements at an intersection may experience a low level-of-service, while the intersection as a whole
may operate acceptably.

In addition to the adjusted 2013 traffic conditions, an analysis was performed for the AM and PM peak
hours for the projected 2017 no-build traffic conditions (background traffic growth), the projected 2017
build conditions (background traffic growth plus the proposed development traffic), and projected 2017
build conditions with improvements. The results of the LOS analysis are summarized in Table 2 and
Table 3 below. A more complete set of the analyses from Synchro is available in Appendix G.

Analyses indicate that McGinnis Ferry Road at Bell Road / Old Atlanta Road currently operates at LOS
D in the adjusted 2013 scenario and LOS E in the 2017 build scenario. Based on the projected 2017
conditions, potential improvements were analyzed at this intersection in accordance with the planned
future projects in the area.

As per Forsyth County's SPLOST VII program, widening the southbound approach allows for the
implementation of dual left-turn lanes and a free flow channelized right turn from McGinnis Ferry onto
Old Atlanta Road. Additionally, by implementing a right-turn overlap on the northbound approach at
this intersection, the level-of-service of this intersection can be improved. The implementation of a
right-turn overlap phase would also require the installation of a "U-Turn Yield to Right Turn" sign for
the westbound approach along McGinnis Ferry. This sign is designated in the Manual on Uniform
Traffic Control Devices (MUTCD) as Sign R 10-16. As indicated in Table 3, these above improvements
result in the McGinnis Ferry at Bell Road intersection operating at or above the level-of-service standard
(LOS D) in the projected 2017 build conditions.

The other signalized intersection at Blackstone Way operates at an acceptable LOS for every current and
future scenario, as does the proposed Site Driveway #3 at Bell Road. The two site driveways along
McGinnis Ferry Road are projected to operate below the acceptable level-of service standard (LOS D),
particularly during the PM peak hours. However, delay is expected at an unsignalized intersection along
a high-volume arterial during the peak hours. The nearby signals are anticipated to create gaps in traffic,
allowing site traffic to exit the site. As indicated in Table 3, an improved scenario was considered for the
intersection of McGinnis Ferry Road and Site Driveway #1 by adding a signal. This scenario improves
the level-of-service of this intersection as well as the right-in/right-out movement at Site Driveway #2.

7.0     CONCLUSION

The McGinnis Ferry Road Tract mixed-use development will consist of approximately 116 single family
homes, 124 townhomes, and 85,000 square-feet of retail/office space (assumed to be approximately
29,000 square-feet of office and 56,000 square-feet of retail). The approximate 51-acre site is located on
the south side of McGinnis Ferry Road just east of Bell Road in the City of Johns Creek, Georgia. The
proposed development is planned to be completed (built-out) by year 2017. At build-out of the
development, access to the site is proposed at two (2) driveways along McGinnis Ferry Road and one
driveway along Bell Road. The study network, composed of three existing intersections and two
additional proposed intersections, was analyzed for 2013 conditions, projected 2017 no-build conditions,
and projected 2017 build conditions.

7.1     General Recommendations

Based on the results of this study in the full build-out condition, Kimley-Horn and Associates, Inc.
recommends the following:

        In accordance with Forsyth County's SPLOST VII, widen Old Atlanta Road at the intersection
        with McGinnis Ferry Road, allowing for dual left-turn lanes on the southbound approach and a
        free flow channelized right turn from westbound McGinnis Ferry onto northbound Old Atlanta
        Road.

        Implement a right-turn overlap phase on the northbound approach of McGinnis Ferry Road and
        Bell Road. Additionally, install MUTCD sign R 10-16, which indicates "U-Turn Yield to Right
        Turn", for the westbound approach along McGinnis Ferry.

        At the intersection of McGinnis Ferry Road and Site Driveway #1 / Shopping Center Driveway,
        install a traffic signal when warranted.

        As per the site plan in Appendix A, provide deceleration lanes for the right-turn movements from
        northbound Bell Road into Site Driveway #3 and from eastbound McGinnis Ferry Road into Site
        Driveway #1 and Site Driveway #2.

        Provide a left-turn lane for the westbound approach along McGinnis Ferry Road at Site
        Driveway #1 / Shopping Center Driveway.

                                   PROJECT DESCRIPTION

Projects WID 0208-1, 0208-2 and 0208-3 are proposed as a major roadway widening and
reconstruction project to provide safety and operational improvements along Old Atlanta Road.
Widening and adjusting the alignment and grade of the roadway is necessary to improve
capacity, reduce traffic accidents and to accommodate a 45 mph design speed based on current
design criteria. These improvements will create a safer and more efficient roadway for the
traveling public. The project will extend from Sharon Road to McGinnis Ferry Road a distance
of approximately 4.9 miles. Projects WID 0208-2 and 0208-3 will be constructed during Phase 1
of the project and will include the widening along Old Atlanta Road between Nichols Road and
James Burgess Road as well as improvements to the intersections themselves. These
intersections will be upgraded to include left and right turn lanes with upgraded signals. Project
WID 0208-1 will be constructed during Phase 2 of the project and will include constructing the
remainder of the widening along Old Atlanta Road from Sharon Road to Nichols Road and from
James Burgess Road to McGinnis Ferry Road. Additional intersection improvements may also
be required to meet traffic demand for future traffic volumes.

The existing facility will be widened from a two-lane rural section to four-lane divided road with a
20-foot raised median. The proposed improvements will include widening, in addition to
horizontal and vertical realignment of the roadway at various locations along the project. The
proposed typical section will provide two 12-foot travel lanes in each direction, a 20-foot raised
median, improved shoulders, curb and gutter and sidewalks along specified roadway segments
and additional turn lanes at major intersections. The outside edge of pavement will consist of an
urban section throughout the project length along Old Atlanta Road. Specific dimensions
describing the travel lane improvements are shown in the Typical Section display. The
construction of the roadway will be completed under traffic with all current through lanes
maintained during construction.

   to make smaller-scale improvements along existing roadways which are the most critical for cross-jurisdictional travel. With the exception of
   certain systemwide programs with broad benefits across a defined geographic area, eligibility under this program is limited to facilities on the
   Regional Strategic Transportation System, with additional priority given to those also identified as a Regional Thoroughfare. Both McFarland Road
