Background
The Federal Highway Administration defines a Road Safety Audit (RSA) as the formal safety
examination of an existing or future road or intersection by an independent, multidisciplinary team. The
purpose of an RSA is to identify potential safety issues and possible opportunities for safety
improvements considering all roadway users. The Old Colony Planning Council (OCPC) requested that
MassHighway conduct a Road Safety Audit of three intersections within the Town of Easton, one of
which is the intersection of Foundry Street (Route 106) and Prospect Street. The intersection has been
identified by OCPC as a high crash location within the Town. As such, an RSA was conducted in order
to identify both short and long term safety improvements that can be made at the subject intersection.


Project Description
The intersection of Foundry Street and Prospect Street, shown in Figure 1, is located in the southern
portion of Easton. Foundry Street and Prospect Street are both Town-owned roadways. Foundry Street is
functionally classified as a Rural Minor Arterial, while Prospect Street is classified as a Local Road.
Foundry Street is an important regional roadway, connecting Easton with Mansfield to the west and West
Bridgewater to the east. Foundry Street provides access to Route 138 and has an interchange with Route
24 to the east. Route 106 runs essentially parallel to I-495, and is known to serve as a cut-through for
vehicles bound for Route 24 and points north.

The intersection of Foundry Street and Prospect Street provides a single lane of travel on all approaches.
In the vicinity of the intersection, Foundry Street lane widths vary from 12 feet to 14 feet in each
direction. Both Prospect Street approaches provide a single 9 foot travel lane in each direction. None of
the approaches have a defined shoulder but
have striped edge lines to delineate the edge
of pavement. There are no sidewalks,
crosswalks or handicap accessible pedestrian
facilities in the vicinity of the intersection.

The posted speed limit on Foundry Street is 30 MPH in the area of the intersection but increases to 40
MPH both east and west of the intersection. Speed data for Foundry Street eastbound provided by OCPC
showed an average speed of 43 MPH and an 85th percentile speed of 47 MPH. The 85th percentile speed
is defined as the speed at which 85 percent of the traffic is traveling at or below. Speed data for Foundry
Street westbound provided by OCPC showed an average speed of 41 MPH and an 85th percentile speed of
44 MPH. Speed data for Foundry Street were recorded inside the posted 30 MPH zone. The posted
speed limit on both legs of Prospect Street is 30 MPH. Speed data for Prospect Street northbound
provided by OCPC showed an average speed of 37 MPH and an 85th percentile speed of 42 MPH. No
data were provided for Prospect Street southbound.

Crash data were provided by the Easton Police Department for the three year period from 2005 through
2007 and are included in the Appendix. There were a total of 13 crashes at the intersection within the
three year period. The data show a prevalence of angle-type collisions where a vehicle from Prospect
Street that was either turning onto or crossing Foundry Street was struck by a through vehicle on Foundry
Street. The crash summary shows that six of the 13 crashes occurred when a vehicle failed to yield the
right of way. OCPC calculated a crash rate of 1.498 crashes per million entering vehicles (MEV) at this
intersection as part of their 2007 Easton State Numbered Routes Study. This is almost three times the
District 5 average of 0.58 for unsignalized intersections.

In addition to the crash summary provided, it is noted that there was a fatality at the intersection in
February 2009 from an angle-type crash between a vehicle traveling on Prospect Street northbound and a
vehicle traveling on Foundry Street eastbound. A campaign has been started in memory of the crash
victim (http://www.seethelight.us/), and as a result the Town of Easton has received close to 5,000
signatures supporting installation of a traffic signal at the intersection.

Automatic Traffic Recorder (ATR) counts and Manual Turning Movement (MTM) counts were provided
by OCPC. ATR data were recorded in 2004 for both Foundry Street and Prospect Street. Foundry Street
carries 10,600 vehicles per day (vpd). Prospect Street varies 2,100 vpd. MTM data were recorded in
2004 for both the AM and PM peak periods. The AM peak hour occurred from 8:00 to 9:00 AM. During
the AM peak hour, there were 21 left turns from Prospect Street southbound to Foundry Street eastbound,
against 611 through vehicles on Foundry Street (336 eastbound, 275 westbound). The PM peak hour
occurred from 5:00 to 6:00 PM. During the PM peak hour, there were 30 left turns from Prospect Street
southbound to Foundry Street eastbound, against 862 through vehicles on Foundry Street (434 eastbound,
428 westbound). There were 85 left turns from Foundry Street westbound to Prospect Street southbound
in the PM peak hours. OCPC performed capacity analysis at this intersection as part of their 2007 Easton
State Numbered Routes Study. The analyses showed that the intersection operates at Level of Service
"C" during the AM peak hour and Level of Service "F" during the afternoon peak hour. Despite
relatively low volumes on Prospect Street, these conflicting volumes, in conjunction with the high speed
of travel on Foundry Street and sight distance deficiencies at the intersection, present challenges for
vehicles exiting Prospect Street.

Audit Observations
Following a brief introduction to the RSA process and a summary of traffic volumes and crash
information, the audit participants were asked to discuss issues that may affect safety at the intersection of
Foundry Street (Route 106) and Prospect Street. Audit participants then conducted a site visit as a group,
at which time they offered observations on safety concerns and deficiencies. A summary of those major
safety considerations is as follows:

            ·    Sight Distance ­ This was the primary concern of all audit participants. Visibility is
                 restricted on both Prospect Street approaches and is severely restricted on the northbound
                 approach. Sight distance is restricted by the horizontal and vertical alignment of Foundry
                 Street and by stone walls and vegetation bordering properties on the southwest and
                 southeast corners. These same obstacles also create sight distance deficiencies for
                 vehicles approaching on Foundry Street.

            ·    Truck Traffic ­ This was a concern of Town of Easton personnel that were part of the
                 audit team. There is an LNG facility to the west which contributes to truck traffic in the
                 area. It was also suggested that trucks use Foundry Street as an alternative to I-495
                 because it provides a more direct route to Route 24 and points north.

            ·    Intersection Avoidance ­ Several audit participants suggested that parents are known to
                 warn young drivers to avoid the intersection, despite the fact that any alternative route
                 would at least double travel time.

            ·    Speed ­ Speed was not brought up as an isolated concern at the intersection, but was
                 discussed in conjunction with sight distance deficiencies at the intersection.


Potential Safety Enhancements
After the site visit, audit participants returned to the meeting location to discuss the safety issues and
consider improvements. Audit participants were encouraged to consider both short and long term
improvements for each issue. Each improvement considered has been categorized as short-term, mid-
term, or long-term based on the definitions shown in Table 2. Additionally, a cost category has been
assigned to each improvement based on the parameters set forth in Table 2.

Sight Distance

The following improvements were proposed by audit participants to alleviate sight distance concerns at
the intersection of Foundry Street and Prospect Street. As previously discussed, sight distance is a
concern on all four approaches of the intersection. Most audit participants thought that sight distance
improvements must be corrected either with or without traffic signal installation. Specific
recommendations are as follows:

    ·   Signalization. The installation of a traffic signal was promoted by most audit participants, and is
        supported by both OCPC and the Town of Easton. In addition, the Town has received nearly
        5,000 signatures in support of traffic signal installation. Installation of a traffic signal is a mid-
        term, high cost improvement. A traffic signal would address sight distance concerns by providing
        a protected phase for Prospect Street traffic. The signal would be fully actuated and would
        require Foundry Street traffic to stop only when a vehicle is present on the Prospect Street
        approaches. From an engineering perspective, a traffic signal cannot be installed without related
        geometric improvements to meet minimum stopping sight distance requirements at the
        intersection. It was also suggested that an illuminated "RED" SIGNAL AHEAD sign be placed
        on Foundry Street eastbound in advance of the signal to notify vehicles in advance of the
        horizontal curve.

    ·   Yellow/Red Flashing Beacon. The installation of a flashing beacon would increase visibility of
        the intersection but would not provide protection for vehicles exiting Prospect Street. Installation
        of a flashing beacon is a mid-term, medium cost improvement. Installation of a flashing beacon
        would improve awareness of the intersection for vehicles approaching on Foundry Street, but
        would not improve sight distance. It was noted by an audit participant that a flashing beacon
        could be installed as an interim measure in advance of a traffic signal.

    ·   Improve horizontal alignment. Prospect Street intersects Foundry Street within a horizontal curve
        on Foundry Street. As such, it severely
        limits sight distance, specifically for
        vehicles exiting from the south, which is
        on the inside of the curve. Foundry Street
        could be realigned to provide a gentler,
        flatter curve through the Prospect Street
        intersection. This would likely require
        land acquisition. This is a long-term, high
        cost improvement.

    ·   Improve vertical alignment. There is a
        crest vertical curve on Foundry Street just
        east of the Prospect Street intersection.

        southeast corner of the intersection which restrict sight distance for northbound vehicles.
        Removal of the wall and/or fence would help improve sight distance but would be most effective
        in conjunction with a lowering of the Foundry Street profile. This improvement would require
        cutting the crest vertical curve and performing related utility work and grading modifications of
        driveways and roadway edge slopes, and may require property acquisition. This is a long-term,
        high cost improvement. It should be noted that the Town lowered the profile of Foundry Street
        approximately 20 years ago to improve sight distance at Prospect Street.

    ·   Property acquisition (Right-of-Way Taking). It was discussed during the audit that property
        acquisition would be required in conjunction with horizontal re-alignment. Property acquisition
        by the Town would be required in conjunction with any sight distance improvement. It should be
        noted that the town built a stone wall on private property on the southwest corner of the
        intersection while cutting away vegetation on the corner in order to gain some improvement in
        visibility for vehicles exiting Prospect Street from the south and for vehicles approaching
        Prospect Street from the west. This is a mid-term, high cost improvement that would only be
        undertaken in conjunction with geometric improvements.

    ·    Improve sign visibility. There are currently "DANGEROUS
        INTERSECTION AHEAD" signs and standard W2-1 Intersection
        Warning Signs on all four approaches at the intersection. It was noted
        that these signs are obstructed by overgrowth on the Foundry Street
        approach. Tree and shrub trimming should be done on all four
        approaches to insure that the signs are clearly visible for approaching
        vehicles. This is a short-term, low cost improvement.

    ·   Install "Stop Ahead" signs. On the Prospect Street approaches, the
        "DANGEROUS INTERSECTION AHEAD" signs and standard W2-1
        Intersection Warning Signs on both approaches warn vehicles of the
        intersection but do not warn that a stop is required. W3-1a "STOP
        AHEAD" graphic signs should be added on both Prospect Street
        approaches to confirm that approaching vehicles need to stop. This is a
        short-term, low cost improvement.

    ·   Move poles. There are utility poles along the north side of Foundry Street east of Prospect Street
        and along the south side of Foundry Street west of Prospect Street. These poles create a sight
        distance obstruction, specifically for northbound vehicles on Prospect Street looking west. The
        poles could be set further back so as not to impact the sight triangle at the intersection. This
        improvement would likely require property acquisition or a utility easement along Foundry
        Street, and therefore is a long-term, high cost improvement. This improvement may impact
        existing street lights, which are located on the poles closest to the intersection.

Truck Traffic

Truck traffic was presented as a safety concern at the intersection. Trucks are known to travel north on
Bay Road from I-495 to the Five Corners intersection in Easton, which is approximately 1.5 miles west of

Prospect Street on Foundry Street. They then travel along Route 106 through the study intersection to
Route 24. This is a shorter route than the highway route of I-495 to Route 24. Although the cut-through
nature of truck traffic was suggested by several audit participants, no specific data were presented to
indicate routes traveled by trucks. It is recommended that an origin-destination study be performed by
OCPC or by the Town to determine travel routes of truck traffic. As part of the origin-destination study,
truck routes can be examined at a regional level to determine if acceptable alternate routes exist. It should
be noted that Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) tankers have been observed on Foundry Street in the area, and
that they often use specific routes that have been laid out based on operational and security concerns.
This is a mid-term solution with no cost in physical modifications.

Education

Until physical improvements can be made, education can be used to increase awareness of the safety
concerns at the intersection. The Easton Police Department expressed a willingness to work with students
at the nearby Southeastern Regional Vocational High School in order to educate young drivers to exercise
safe driving habits throughout Easton and specifically in the vicinity of the school. The Police
Department can also promote alternate routes as part of this education initiative. This is a short-term, low
cost solution.

Speed

Speed was not brought up as a specific concern during the audit meeting, but was discussed in
conjunction with the other physical deficiencies at the intersection. Additional recommendations to
alleviate speed concerns at the intersection are as follows:

    ·   Enforcement. The Easton Police Department noted that they frequently enforce speed limits
        along Foundry Street, specifically in the area of Prospect Street. Enforcement measures should
        continue in this area. This is a low cost, short term improvement.

    ·   Install "Speed Reduction" signage. The speed limit along Foundry
        Street is 40 MPH except in the immediate vicinity of Prospect Street,
        where it reduces to 30 MPH. W3-5 "Speed Reduction" warning signs
        should be installed in advance of the 30 MPH zone to raise driver
        awareness of the reduction in speed limit. This is a low cost, short term
        improvement.
